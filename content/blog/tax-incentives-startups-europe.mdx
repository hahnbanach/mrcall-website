The European Union has been encouraging private investment in technology and innovative startups for years, recognizing them as an engine of economic growth and competitiveness. While there is no single tax regime at the EU level (direct taxation is a national competence), many member states have introduced targeted tax incentives for those investing in the capital of innovative startups, in compliance with EU regulations on state aid.

## Italy

In Italy, the discipline of innovative startups (introduced by Decree Law 179/2012 and subsequent amendments) provides substantial tax incentives for those who invest in the capital of these companies.

**Individuals (IRPEF)** - Individual investors can deduct from their IRPEF up to 50 percent of the amount invested in the capital of an innovative startup or innovative SME. This deduction is applicable on a maximum investment of €100,000 per year for startups (maximum deduction €50,000) and €300,000 for innovative SMEs (maximum deduction €150,000). The investment must be maintained for at least 3 years. From 2025, the IRPEF deduction will rise to 65 percent for investments in startups made by individuals, thanks to the Centemero Law 2024. In addition, capital gain exemption is introduced for individuals who dispose of investments after at least 3 years.

**Legal persons (IRES)** - Corporate entities benefit from concessions: previously there was a deduction from taxable income equal to 30 percent of the investment in innovative startups (up to €1.8 million) for investing companies. With new rules, the aim is to introduce a tax credit for institutional investors (companies, funds, incubators). For certified incubators and accelerators, a specific tax credit has been established to help finance startups. Corporate holdings in innovative startups also enjoy the ordinary regime of partial exemption of capital gains (95 percent) in case of disposal after 12 months.

**Conditions**: The startup must be registered in the special section of the Companies Register as an "innovative startup." The subsidized investment must be maintained for at least 3 years; IRPEF deduction at 50-65% is granted within the EU de minimis ceiling of €200,000.

## Portugal

Portugal has introduced tax incentives for so-called "investidores anjo" (business angels) as part of the Startup Portugal strategy. Specifically, the 2017 Budget Law created the "Programa Semente" ("Seed Program") to encourage investment in early-stage startups.

**Individuals (IRS)** - Individuals who invest in the capital of an innovative startup are eligible for an income tax credit (IRS) equal to 25 percent of the investment made. This benefit is recognized in the form of a deduction from the IRS due. The eligible investment is subject to caps: it can be considered up to €100,000 per year per taxpayer, and must result in a stake of no more than 30 percent of the share capital or voting rights of the funded startup. In practice, an investor can deduct up to €25,000 per year. The regulations also require that the investment be in cash (capital contribution) and that the beneficiary startup meet the requirements of an innovative small business.

**Legal Persons (IRC)** - There is no equivalent direct tax incentive for companies investing in startups. However, Portugal does favor investments through venture capital funds and investment companies: income from investments made through approved venture capital funds may enjoy exemptions or reduced rates. Recent reforms have updated the tax regime for alternative venture capital investment vehicles. In addition, investing in startups can contribute to obtaining the Golden Visa (residency permit for foreign investors), for example, by investing at least €500,000 in Portuguese venture capital funds.

**Conditions:** Programa Semente applies to investments in startups certified as innovative (often newborn or newly formed) approved by the public agency IAPMEI. The investor must have no pre-existing shareholding ties with the company and must maintain the stake for a minimum period (indicatively 3 years).

## Spain

Spain recently passed Ley 28/2022 de fomento del ecosistema de las empresas emergentes (known as the "Ley de Startups"), which went into effect in 2023, significantly boosting tax incentives for investing in startups.

**Individuals (IRPF)** - Individual investors (business angels, founding partners or other individuals) who invest in the capital of a new innovative enterprise benefit from an IRPF deduction of 50 percent of the amount invested. The Startup Law 2022 raised the deduction from the previous 30 percent to 50 percent, and doubled the maximum taxable base from €60,000 to €100,000 annually. This means that an investor can get up to €50,000 in tax relief per year by investing in startups. The period within which the investment can be made from the startup is extended from 3 to 5 years (or 7 years for startups in biotech, energy, industrial and other strategic sectors). The investor's participation cannot exceed 40 percent of the startup's share capital (also considering family members), with the exception of founders: with the new law, founders can also take advantage of the deduction, regardless of the share of capital owned. The capital gain exemption is confirmed: if the investor sells his shares after at least 3 years (and within 12 years) and reinvests the proceeds in other startups, the capital gain is tax-free.

**Legal Persons (Impuesto de Sociedades)** - The main change for investing companies concerns the regime for sociedades de capital-riesgo (venture capital funds and corporate incubators) and the treatment for startups themselves. Certified startups themselves enjoy a reduced I.S. rate of 15 percent (instead of 25 percent) for the first four fiscal years. There is no specific tax deduction for an outside company investing in a startup, however, approved startup investment companies (VC funds, Sociedades de Inversion en Entidades de Capital Riesgo - SGEICs) enjoy a favorable tax regime. In addition, the new law introduced a discipline to favor corporate venture capital: dividends and capital gains received by companies investing in innovative startups can fall under the normal partial participation exemption (Spanish participation exemption regime, which exempts at 95% income from qualified holdings held >1 year). Finally, Spain's autonomous regions may provide additional incentives: for example, Catalonia, Madrid, Aragon and others offer additional IRPF deductions (typically 20-30%) for investments in local startups, which can be combined with the state deduction (within certain limits).

**Conditions**: For a company to qualify as an "empresa emergente" under Ley 28/2022, it must have fewer than 5 years of operation (7 in special sectors), be based in Spain, have annual revenues under €10 million, not distribute profits, have an innovative character, and obtain certification as a "startup" from ENISA. The deductible investment must be made at company formation or through capital increase within 5 (or 7) years of foundation. Shares/stakes acquired must be maintained for at least 3 years and no more than 12 years. The individual investor cannot hold more than 40% of the company (except if they are a founder).

## France

France has long incentivized investment in innovative SMEs and startups with various tax instruments, mainly aimed at natural persons "business angels." The key scheme is known as dispositif Madelin - IR-PME, and recently enhanced for jeunes entreprises innovantes (JEI).

**Individuals (IR)** - A French taxpayer who subscribes capital in an unlisted SME can benefit from an income tax reduction (reduction IR-PME) equal to 18% of the amounts invested, within a limit of €50,000 invested (single) or €100,000 (couples). This is the basic "Madelin" relief. In recent years, to further stimulate startups, the legislator has introduced increased rates: for example, between 2020 and 2022 the rate was temporarily raised to 25% (with EU authorization) for all SME investments. From 2023, Law 2024 has created a specific income tax reduction of 30% for investments made in Jeunes Entreprises Innovantes (JEI) during the period 2024-2028. In this case, the annual deductible maximum also increases: up to €75,000 (single) or €150,000 (couple) if the investment is in a qualified JEI. In summary, a private investor in 2025 can deduct 30% of what they invest in a qualified innovative startup JEI (within the stated limits). It should be noted that to obtain the benefit, the investor must maintain the new shares for at least 5 years and that the target company must meet strict qualification criteria (SME <10 years, innovative, independent, with at least 2 employees, etc.). Additional incentives for individuals include: exemption from Impot sur la Fortune Immobiliere (former property tax) for holdings in innovative SMEs up to 50% of the investment; and the possibility of subscribing to shares of specialized VC funds (FIP, FCPI) with income tax reduction of 18-30% depending on the case.

**Legal Persons (IS)** - Companies investing in startups do not enjoy a direct deduction on the Impot sur les Societes for capital contributions, except for specific sectoral mechanisms. However, France encourages corporate investment in innovation by other means: for example, Jeune Entreprise Innovante (JEI) status grants startups themselves important tax exemptions (total exemption from corporate tax for the first results and reduced by 50 percent for the following year, exemption from contributions on researchers, etc.), making their financing more attractive. In addition, large corporations can invest through fonds communs de placement dans l'innovation (FCPI) or directly through Societes de Capital-Risque (SCR): the latter are special purpose vehicles dedicated to venture capital, which if they meet portfolio requirements (at least 50 percent in qualifying unlisted SMEs) enjoy a 90 percent exemption on capital gains from the sale of participations and dividends received. In practice, a corporate can create or invest in an SCR to finance startups and take advantage of a quasi-exempt regime on returns on such investments (only 10 percent of distributable profits are taxed). There is also a Recherche Tax Credit (CIR) for companies that finance external R&D projects, which can be used if they finance startups for research activities.

**Conditions:** For the IR-PME reduction, the recipient company must be an eligible SME: independent European SMI, active for <10 years, subject to IS in France, with commercial/industrial or innovative activity (not financial or real estate), and not listed. The investor must not hold >25% of the capital of the target company (before and after the operation) and must retain the shares for at least 5 years. The IR-PME benefit is subject to annual capping and EU state aid regulations.

## Ireland

Ireland offers tax incentives for both individual and corporate investors aimed at supporting innovative start-ups and stimulating domestic venture capital. The two main instruments for individuals are the Employment and Investment Incentive (EII) and the Start-Up Relief for Entrepreneurs (SURE).

**Individuals (Income Tax)** - With the Employment and Investment Incentive an individual who invests in a qualified unlisted Irish company (typically a young SME, business or technology activity) can get an income tax reduction of up to 40% of the amount invested. In practice, 30 percent of the investment is deductible immediately from Irish IRPEF (Income Tax) and an additional 10 percent becomes deductible after holding the shares for at least 4 years (provided the funded company has met certain employment or expenditure targets of the funds raised). The maximum investment allowed in a tax year per individual investor is usually €250,000 (with a possibility of €500,000 if the investment is "transferred" over several years). This means that an investor who commits €250k to a startup could reduce his or her tax liability by up to €100k over the period. The EII scheme requires that the purchased shares be held for at least 4 years. In parallel, the SURE program allows entrepreneurs (people who start their own startup after working as employees) to recover part of the income tax paid in previous years: by investing capital in the new company, the founder can get a tax refund of up to 41% of the investment (within cumulative caps around €100,000 annually, up to ~€700,000 total). In addition, Ireland provides capital gains exemption for reinvestments: if an individual realizes a capital gain by selling equity investments and reinvests it in new EII startups, they can get "capital gains tax relief," i.e., suspension/exemption of taxation on that reinvested capital gain.

**Legal Persons (Corporate Tax)** - There are no tax credits dedicated exclusively to companies investing in startups. However, companies making strategic investments in startups can benefit from general schemes: for example, Ireland allows full exemption from capital gains taxation for qualifying holdings (holdings of at least 5 percent for at least 12 months in trading companies active in the EU). This means that a parent company that finances a startup and resells its shares after a certain period does not pay Capital Gains Tax on any gains, under ordinary rules. In addition, there are strong incentives for R&D activities (25 percent tax credit on research expenses) and a "Knowledge Development Box" regime with effective tax rate at 6.25 percent on income from developed patents and IP. Ireland also encourages the formation of seed capital and VC funds through public-funded programs (Enterprise Ireland and the Seed & Venture Capital Scheme).

**Conditions:** The EII scheme requires the beneficiary company to be an unlisted Irish SME, independent and operating in qualified sectors (passive, financial or real estate activities are excluded). The investor must have no employment ties with the company (unless it uses SURE as a promoter). The maximum deduction (250k) is subject to the general limit on investment tax reliefs. Shares must be common, subscribed at new and held 4 years.

## United Kingdom

The United Kingdom has one of the most established systems of tax incentives for investment in innovative startups and SMEs. The mainstays are the Enterprise Investment Scheme (EIS) and the Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme (SEIS), introduced in 1994 and 2012, respectively, as well as Venture Capital Trusts (VCTs).

**Individuals (Income Tax and Capital Gains)** - With the Enterprise Investment Scheme an individual investor gets an income tax deduction equal to 30 percent of the amount invested in shares of a qualified EIS company. One can invest up to £1,000,000 per year (or £2,000,000 if at least half of the investment is in "knowledge-intensive" enterprises), so the maximum annual benefit is £300,000 tax reduction. Shares must be held for at least 3 years; if complied with, the entire gain realized on sale is exempt from Capital Gains Tax. In addition, any losses can be deducted from taxes. The EIS also allows capital gains to be deferred: if the taxpayer has realized a capital gain by selling other assets, they can invest it in an EIS company within 1 year earlier or 3 years later and defer the taxation of that gain (deferral relief). The Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme is targeted at seed startups (very small, up to 2 years old): it offers an even larger deduction, 50% of the investment in IRPEF tax credit. The annual SEIS investment limit is £200,000 (following an increase in 2023), with a maximum relief then of £100,000/year. Earnings on SEIS shares held 3 years are also exempt from taxation, and special reinvestment relief applies: by reinvesting within the same tax year a capital gain in a SEIS company, 50% of that investment (up to £100k) goes exempt from Capital Gains Tax. Finally, Venture Capital Trusts are listed funds that invest in startups: by subscribing to shares in a VCT, an individual enjoys 30% tax relief (up to £200k invested) and any dividends and capital gains from the VCT are exempt.

**Legal Persons (Corporation Tax)** - There is currently no tax deduction scheme comparable to EIS/SEIS for companies investing in startups. In the past, the UK had experimented with a Corporate Venturing Scheme (CVS) that gave companies a 20 percent relief on investments in innovative SMEs, but this scheme was abolished in 2010 due to low utilization. Today, companies that want to invest in startups usually do so through VC funds or dedicated vehicles. However, from a general tax point of view, it should be noted that UK companies benefit from a broad exemption for substantial shareholdings: a UK holding company that holds >10% of a trading company for at least 12 months can sell the holding free of Corporation Tax on capital gains. This, combined with a relatively low corporate tax rate, still makes it worthwhile for companies and funds to invest in startup equity. In addition, companies can deduct as a tax expense any losses on minority interests not covered by the participation exemption regime, and they can take advantage of the generous 13 percent R&D tax credit (or 130 percent super-deduction) if they collaborate with technology startups on research projects.

**Conditions:** EIS and SEIS require strict criteria for the beneficiary company (qualification as "qualifying trading company"): limit to gross assets (<£15 million for EIS, <£350k for SEIS), <250 employees (EIS), excluded sectors (finance, property, energy already subsidized, etc.), use of collected funds for growth and development, not mere replacement of other capital. Each company can raise up to £5 million/year (EIS/VCT combined) and a total of £12 million in life (more if knowledge-intensive). The investor cannot be "connected" to the company (no holdings >30% or be employee administrator, etc.) otherwise they lose the right to relief.

## Germany

In Germany, tax incentives for investments in startups have developed mainly in the form of direct subsidies and exemptions rather than tax deductions. The pivotal program is INVEST - Zuschuss fur Wagniskapital, launched by the Federal Ministry of Economics in 2013 and still operational, aimed at supporting business angels who finance innovative startups.

**Individuals (Business Angels)** - The INVEST program grants a private investor who buys stakes in a young innovative company a cash refund of 20 percent of what is invested (in the form of a non-repayable state grant). In practice, if an angel invests €100,000 in a qualified German startup, they can receive a bonus of €20,000 from the state, effectively reducing the net cost of the investment. The maximum amount considered per startup is €500,000 per year, so the bonus can be up to €100,000 per investor per individual company. In addition, to make the exit more attractive, the program provides a capital gains tax exemption: if the investor resells their stake after at least 3 years, the capital gain realized is exempt from capital gains tax (Effektenertragsteuer) up to a certain amount, through an additional "exit" bonus equivalent to the tax that would be due. This effectively resets the taxation on the gain to zero in the event of a successful exit after the minimum period. Such measures are authorized because they fall under EU de minimis aid and as an exception to ordinary rules (in Germany, gains from the sale of company shares by individuals would otherwise be taxed at 25 percent as capital gains). Note that the INVEST bonus does not affect the investor's tax return (it is not a tax deduction but a separate disbursement from BAFA, the federal agency that administers the program).

**Legal Persons (Corporate and Funds)** - Germany does not offer a dedicated tax incentive for corporations that invest directly in startups. However, the German tax system provides that capital gains on corporate holdings are largely exempt for corporations: a corporation that disposes of a shareholding in another company pays tax on only 5% of the gain (95% is exempt), provided the holding was >=10% and held for at least 1 year. This general regime (Teileinkuenfteverfahren) also favors corporate investments in startups, as any successful exit is minimally taxed in the hands of the investing company. In addition, to stimulate venture capital, Germany has created public co-investment funds (e.g., High-Tech Grunderfonds) and from 2021 the Zukunftsfonds (Future Fund) with €10 billion public to co-invest with private individuals: by investing through these vehicles, institutions can obtain matching funding and indirectly reduce risk. In contrast, there are no specific tax deductions for banks or corporations that finance startups, other than the normal mechanisms for deducting any capital losses.

**Conditions:** The INVEST grant is reserved for investments in startups based in Germany (or EU but with significant activity in Germany) that are less than 7 years old, have innovative or technological character (certified through specific criteria of innovativeness or belonging to high-tech sectors) and no more than 50 employees. The minimum investment to access the bonus is €10,000, and the investor cannot already hold more than 50 percent of the company. The stake must be held for at least 3 years (holding period) in order not to return the bonus. Each investor can receive bonuses for investments on multiple startups up to €3 million invested/year.

## Poland

Poland has recently introduced, in the tax reform known as the "Polski Lad" (New Polish Order, Budget Law 2022), a number of tax breaks to stimulate investment in innovative companies. Specifically, "ulgi" (deductions/relief) are provided for both individuals and companies investing through venture capital vehicles or directly in startups.

**Individuals and legal entities - Venture Capital (PIT/CIT):** The main benefit introduced in 2022 is the "ulga na inwestycje Venture Capital," applicable to both IRPEF and IRES taxpayers. It allows a taxpayer to deduct from their taxable income 50 percent of the cost of acquiring shares in a qualified alternatywna spolka inwestycyjna (ASI) or directly in a target company provided the latter has at least 5 percent of the shares held by an ASI. In practice, an investor (individual or corporation) who invests, for example, PLN 1,000,000 through a Polish venture capital fund (ASI) in startups can deduct PLN 500,000 as an additional deductible cost from their taxable income. The actual tax savings will depend on the marginal tax rate (19% flat tax for corporations, up to 32% for individuals), but the measure is a significant incentive. The deduction is limited to PLN 250,000 per year of deductible amount maximum, thus corresponding to investments of PLN 500,000 per year per taxpayer. The investment must be maintained for at least 2 years (24 months) in order not to lose the benefit. This ulga is particularly designed for those who invest in startups through local VC funds or similar vehicles - thus incentivizing both business angels (who can create their own ASI) and companies that allocate cash in venture funds.

**Other facilities:** In addition to the aforementioned VC ulga, Poland has introduced other related measures: for example, an ulga "na konsolidacje" that allows a company acquiring a majority stake in a target company (e.g., innovative scale-up) to deduct transaction costs of up to PLN 250,000. There is also an IPO ulga to incentivize the listing of innovative SMEs on the stock exchange and an ulga na ekspansje for expansion expenses in new markets. For individual investors who sell a stake in a startup, there is the possibility of capital gain exemption by reinvesting in other innovative companies (a criterion similar to the Spanish rollover), although the framework is not yet as complete as in other countries. Also, on the startup front, Poland offers a reduction of the CIT rate to 5 percent on IP income (IP Box) and an optional "Estonian CIT" (taxation upon profit distribution) regime for reinvesting companies, which can include startups. These measures indirectly encourage investment because they make the internal growth of startups more tax efficient.

**Conditions:** The Venture Capital ulga requires that the ASI through which one invests be a vehicle authorized under Polish law (equivalent to closed-end investment funds or EUVECA) and independent of the investor (the investor cannot already be affiliated with the fund or target company in the previous two years). The investor must maintain the investment for at least 2 years. The target company must be an unlisted spolka z o.o. or S.A. that is innovative or otherwise qualified according to innovative SME criteria.
